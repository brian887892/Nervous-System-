1 What are the two subdivisions of the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)?
✓ answer: The Somatic Nervous System (SNS) and the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS).
2 List the six major anatomical divisions of the brain, from the highest to the lowest level of information processing, as presented in the text.
✓ answer: Cerebrum, Diencephalon, Midbrain, Cerebellum, Pons, Medulla Oblongata.
3 What are the five lobes of the cerebrum?
✓ answer: Frontal, Parietal, Temporal, Occipital, and Insula.
4 Describe the primary function of the frontal lobe.
✓ answer: It is concerned with higher intellectual functions, behavioral aspects, and voluntary control of skeletal muscle movement.
5 What is the difference between a sulcus and a fissure?
✓ answer: A sulcus is a groove on the surface of the cerebrum, while a fissure is a deep groove that separates hemispheres.
6 Name the three kinds of cerebral functional areas.
✓ answer: Sensory areas, Association areas, and Motor areas.
7 If a patient has difficulty with spatial perception and artistic endeavors, which cerebral hemisphere might be affected?
✓ answer: The Right Hemisphere.
8 What is the primary function of association fibers in the brain?
✓ answer: To connect different cortical areas on the same side (within the same hemisphere) of the cerebrum.
9 What is the main role of the Reticular Activating System (RAS)?
✓ answer: To maintain consciousness and alertness, and function in sleep and arousal from sleep.
10 Name three structures that are part of the limbic system.
✓ answer: Cingulate Gyrus, Hippocampus, Amygdala. (Any three are acceptable)
11 What is neuroplasticity, as described in the context of the hippocampus?
✓ answer: The ability of the brain to create and reorganize new synaptic connections, especially in response to learning or after injury.
12 The amygdala is involved in processing which two types of emotional memories?
✓ answer: Threatening stimuli (fear) and positive emotional memories (e.g., recognizing a loved one's face).
13 What are the three parts of the diencephalon?
✓ answer: Epithalamus, Thalamus, and Hypothalamus.
14 The hypothalamus controls the Autonomic Nervous System. Give two examples of functions it regulates this way.
✓ answer: Heart rate, blood pressure, bronchiole diameter, sweat glands, G.I. tract activity. (Any two are acceptable)
15 When the body's temperature rises above the set point, which center in the hypothalamus is activated, and what are its two effects?
✓ answer: The Heat-losing Center is activated, causing sweating and cutaneous vasodilation.
16 What is the function of osmoreceptors in the hypothalamus?
✓ answer: They detect changes in the osmotic pressure of blood and extracellular fluid to help stimulate the thirst response.
17 The substantia nigra is located in which part of the brain, and what is its primary function?
✓ answer: It is located in the midbrain, and its function is to coordinate and control body movement.
18 What are the two pairs of bodies that make up the corpora quadrigemina?
✓ answer: The superior colliculi and the inferior colliculi.
19 If you automatically turn your head towards a loud, unexpected sound, which part of your midbrain is responsible for this reflex?
✓ answer: The inferior colliculi.
20 Define the role of proprioceptors.
✓ answer: They are sensory receptors located in joints, muscles, tendons, and ligaments that deliver information regarding the relative position of various body parts to the cerebellum.
21 Explain how the cerebellum helps you maintain your balance if you trip.
✓ answer: It receives information from proprioceptors about the body's position and coordinates rapid, automatic adjustments of body parts to re-establish equilibrium.
22 What is the function of the middle cerebellar peduncle?
✓ answer: It connects the cerebellum to the pons, relaying input from the cerebral cortex to the cerebellum.
23 The pons is involved in regulating respiration. What specific aspect of breathing does it control?
✓ answer: It controls the changes in depth of breathing and the fine-tuning of the rhythm set by the medulla.
24 List the four cranial nerves that originate from the pons-medullary junction.
✓ answer: Abducens (VI), Facial (VII), and Vestibulocochlear (VIII). (Note: The text groups these three at the junction, with the Trigeminal (V) originating from the pons proper).
25 What are the three vital centers located in the medulla oblongata?
✓ answer: The Cardiac Center, the Vasomotor Center, and the Respiratory Center.
26 What is the function of the vasomotor center?
✓ answer: It regulates the diameter of peripheral blood vessels, thereby influencing systemic blood pressure and blood flow.
27 Besides the vital centers, name three protective reflexes that the medulla oblongata helps regulate.
✓ answer: Sneezing, coughing, hiccupping, swallowing, and vomiting. (Any three are acceptable)
28 A patient presents with an inability to shrug their shoulders or turn their head. Which cranial nerve is most likely damaged?
✓ answer: The Accessory nerve (XI).
29 What is a nerve plexus?
✓ answer: A network (rearrangement) of spinal nerves formed when ventral rami join each other and then split again.
30 Name the four main plexuses of spinal nerves.
✓ answer: Cervical, Brachial, Lumbar, and Sacral.
31 Which nerve, arising from the brachial plexus, innervates the extensor muscles of the posterior upper arm and forearm (like the triceps)?
✓ answer: The Radial nerve.
32 The femoral nerve arises from which plexus, and what muscle group does it innervate?
✓ answer: It arises from the Lumbar plexus and innervates the anterior muscles of the thigh and the extensor muscles of the leg.
33 Describe the composition of the sciatic nerve.
✓ answer: It is composed of two nerves (the tibial and common peroneal) encased within a single sheath.
34 What are the three layers of the cranial meninges, from superficial to deep?
✓ answer: Dura mater, Arachnoid mater, Pia mater.
35 Besides the ventricles, name two other locations in the CNS where cerebrospinal fluid is found.
✓ answer: In the subarachnoid space and in the central canal of the spinal cord.
36 What is the function of the choroid plexuses?
✓ answer: They are networks of blood vessels that filter blood to create cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).
37 Describe the pathway of CSF from the third ventricle to the subarachnoid space.
✓ answer: From the 3rd ventricle, it flows through the cerebral aqueduct to the 4th ventricle, and then exits the 4th ventricle through apertures into the subarachnoid space.
38 What are arachnoid villi (or granulations) and what is their function?
✓ answer: They are connections from the subarachnoid space into the dural venous sinuses that allow CSF to return to the venous blood supply.
39 How does CSF provide buoyancy to the brain?
✓ answer: The salty CSF allows the brain to be suspended, reducing its effective weight from about 1400 grams to about 25 grams, preventing its weight from cutting off blood supply.
40 What causes hydrocephalus?
✓ answer: An abnormal accumulation of CSF in the ventricles or subarachnoid space, caused by impaired flow, impaired re-absorption, or excessive production of CSF.
41 Why can an infant's head enlarge significantly in cases of hydrocephalus, whereas an adult's cannot?
✓ answer: Because an infant's skull still has fontanels (soft spots) that allow for expansion, while an adult's skull is fused and rigid.
42 What is the function of the falx cerebelli?
✓ answer: It is a small triangular fold of dura mater that separates the two cerebellar hemispheres.
43 The text mentions that the hippocampus is "plastic." What does this mean in the context of the brain?
✓ answer: It refers to neuroplasticity, the ability of the brain to create and reorganize new synaptic connections.
44 What is the functional difference between the precentral gyrus and the postcentral gyrus?
✓ answer: The precentral gyrus (on the frontal lobe) is the primary motor cortex, controlling movement. The postcentral gyrus (on the parietal lobe) is the primary somatosensory cortex, receiving sensory information.
45 What effect does blue light from screens have on the body's ability to prepare for sleep?
✓ answer: It blocks the hormone melatonin, thus suppressing the body's ability to prepare for sleep.
46 If a patient has a stroke that damages their left cerebral hemisphere, which side of their body would most likely show motor deficits, and why?
✓ answer: The right side of the body, because the left hemisphere controls movement of the right side.
47 The medulla oblongata monitors blood chemistry. What specific aspect of blood chemistry does it monitor to regulate respiration?
✓ answer: It monitors blood acidity.
48 What is the role of the rami communicantes?
✓ answer: They are branches that connect spinal nerves to a sympathetic ganglion, which is part of the autonomic nervous system.
49 The hypothalamus contains a feeding center and a satiety center. How do these two centers interact to regulate hunger?
✓ answer: The feeding center is always active and stimulates hunger, while the satiety center, when stimulated by being satisfied, inhibits the feeding center.
50 What is the unique feature of the neurons in the substantia nigra, and what happens if they degenerate?
✓ answer: They are dopaminergic (release dopamine). If 60-80% of them degenerate, it may result in Parkinson's disease, characterized by loss of motor control and coordination.
